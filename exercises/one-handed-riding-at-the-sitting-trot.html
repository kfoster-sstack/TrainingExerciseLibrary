One-Handed Riding at the Sitting Trot

Focus: Balance, Independence, Steering

Equipment: Western saddle pad

Arena Diagram

Step-by-Step Instructions

  1. At the sitting trot (western slow sitting trot), hold the reins in one hand with your free hand resting on your thigh.
  2. Ride the arena perimeter using only neck rein cues to steer through corners.
  3. Your seat and legs do most of the work. The rein hand provides subtle direction changes.
  4. Practice circles, serpentines, and direction changes at the sitting trot with one hand.
  5. Your free hand should remain quiet — no arm waving or gripping the saddle horn.
  6. Focus on sitting deep and quiet in the saddle. The sitting trot should feel effortless.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Bouncing at the jog because you're sitting too stiff — relax your hips and lower back.
  • Pulling on the reins to slow down instead of using your seat (sit deeper, close your core).
  • The free hand wandering and creating balance issues.
  • The horse speeding up because the rider has less control with one hand.

Variations & Progressions

  • Add loping with one hand for advanced riders.
  • Practice a simple pattern: circle, diagonal, halt, back.
  • Work with zero rein contact for short stretches to test true independence.
Tip: Western riding is about subtlety. The less you do with your hands, the more your horse responds to your seat and legs.
