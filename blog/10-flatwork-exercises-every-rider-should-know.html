10 Flatwork Exercises Every Rider Should Know

By Schneider Saddlery • May 27, 2026 • 8 min read

Flatwork is the language every horse should speak before you ask anything else of him. A jumper who hasn't been schooled on the flat will pull on the reins and rush fences. A western horse who hasn't been schooled on the flat will brace through a spin. A trail horse who hasn't been schooled on the flat will refuse a gate. The hours you put into flatwork are the foundation underneath everything else.

Here are ten flatwork exercises every rider should know, regardless of discipline. Each one transfers. Each one builds something specific you'll feel in your "real" work later. Each one links to a full step-by-step entry in our library.

1. The 20-meter circle

The most fundamental flatwork shape in existence. Ridden correctly, a true 20-meter circle teaches you to think about your horse's whole body — inside leg to outside rein, the outside hind tracking up under the body, the bend uniform from poll to tail. Ridden poorly, it teaches you to drift, pull on the inside rein, and hollow your horse out. Master this and everything else gets easier.

View: 20-Meter Circle Precision →

2. The figure eight

Two 20-meter circles joined at the center. This teaches you to change bend smoothly, ride straight strides between the changes, and keep both circles the same size. If you can't ride a clean figure eight at the trot, you cannot ride a clean change of lead at the canter — the mechanics are the same.

View: Figure-Eight Circles →

3. The serpentine

Three loops down the long side of the arena. The serpentine is a stress test for your change-of-bend mechanics, and it makes your horse straight in a way circles alone never will. Ride three loops at the trot until they feel symmetrical, then bump it to four. Then five.

View: Serpentine Three Loops →

4. The leg yield

The first lateral movement every horse should know. Leg yield teaches your horse to move away from your leg without losing forward motion — the prerequisite for everything from a balanced turn on the forehand to a polished side-pass through a gate. Start from the quarter line to the rail.

View: Leg Yield from the Quarter Line →

5. The shoulder-fore

Shoulder-fore is the secret weapon of every good dressage rider, and the reason their horses are so straight. It's a mild positioning of the shoulders just barely off the track — less angle than a shoulder-in — and it aligns the inside hind leg under the body. Use it as a warm-up tool before every ride.

View: Shoulder-Fore at the Trot →

6. The transition at the letter

Pick a letter, pick a transition (walk to trot, trot to canter, trot to walk), and ride the transition at the letter. Not three strides early. Not five strides late. At. This is precision work, and precision is what separates a horse who tunes into your aids from a horse who guesses.

View: Walk-Trot Transitions on the Long Side →

7. The change of rein across the diagonal

Tracking one direction, turn across the diagonal, ride straight, arrive at the new track on the new bend. Sounds simple. It is not. The diagonal line reveals every drift, every dominant shoulder, every habitual lean. Ride it 4–6 times every ride for a month and your horse's straightness will change permanently.

View: Change of Rein Across the Diagonal →

8. The stretchy circle

At the working trot on a 20-meter circle, gradually let the reins slide through your fingers and ask your horse to follow the contact forward and down. Done correctly, your horse stretches into the bit and lifts the back. Done incorrectly, your horse falls onto the forehand and rushes. The stretchy circle is the truest test of whether your horse is really "through."

View: Stretchy Circle at the Trot →

9. The turn on the forehand

From a halt, the horse's front feet stay nearly in place while the hind feet step around. This is the building block of every lateral movement. It's also a practical skill (gate-opening, repositioning at a mounting block) that every horse should know before he's three years old.

View: Turn on the Forehand →

10. The medium trot across the diagonal

Lengthen the stride across the diagonal, collect back to working trot before the corner. This teaches your horse the difference between more energy and more speed — a distinction that will matter on every jumping line, every barrel run, and every dressage test you ever ride.

View: Medium Trot Across the Diagonal →

How to use these ten as a 30-day plan

Pick three exercises from the list. Ride them for a week, repeated twice each ride. The next week, pick three different ones. After 30 days you'll have ridden every exercise on this list at least three times. Your horse will feel different. He will be straighter, more responsive to your leg, more honest about going forward.
