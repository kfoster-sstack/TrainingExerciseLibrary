Cross-Training Exercises for Western Horses: Why Your Reiner Needs Flatwork

By Schneider Saddlery • May 27, 2026 • 8 min read

The best western horses you'll ever sit on share a secret: they were trained on the flat. Not in a snaffle with a German training program, necessarily — but with attention to the same things English riders obsess over. Even bend. Forward energy. Lateral suppleness. Adjustable canter. A horse who softly answers the bit instead of bracing against it.

This is not a controversial position among professional western trainers. It is controversial among amateur western riders, because somewhere along the line "cross-training" got conflated with "treating my horse like he's not a western horse." That's not what this is. The exercises below are barn drills — they don't change what discipline you ride, they make your western horse better at being a western horse.

Here's why English flatwork helps every western horse, the five exercises worth borrowing, and how to weave them into a 5-day western schedule.

Why western horses benefit from English flatwork

Three reasons:

  1. Western performance requires lateral suppleness. A spin is a lateral movement. A side-pass is a lateral movement. A pleasure-class turn is a lateral movement. Horses who can't bend through the body fall onto the inside shoulder and lose accuracy. English lateral work (shoulder-in, leg-yield, half-pass) builds the muscle memory.
  2. Western performance requires an adjustable canter. A pleasure jog and a barrel-race rate are the same skill in different gears: I ask, my horse adjusts. English transition work teaches the horse this distinction.
  3. Western performance requires straightness. Reining patterns, ranch horse patterns, and trail patterns all require the horse to ride straight lines. Most western horses drift on one direction because no one ever asked them to ride a true center line. English straightness work fixes this in three weeks.

Five English exercises every western horse should ride

1. The 20-meter circle (yes, even for a pleasure horse)

Ride a true 20-meter circle at the trot. Both directions. Focus on even bend. This is the most basic flatwork exercise in existence and the one that exposes the most problems in western horses, who often spend their whole lives going one direction on the rail and never learning to bend symmetrically.

2. Leg yield

Borrowed from dressage, this is the exercise that teaches your horse to move sideways while moving forward. Every western maneuver that involves "give me the shoulder" or "give me the hip" gets easier when the horse already understands leg yield. Start from the quarter line to the rail at the walk, then trot.

3. Walk-trot transitions at every letter

If your western horse can't transition crisply at a specific spot in the arena, he doesn't really understand your aids — he's reacting to your habit. Setting an arena marker (cone, letter, fence post) and demanding a transition at the marker rewires this in a couple of weeks.

View: Walk-Trot Transitions on the Long Side →

4. Shoulder-fore

The most useful position in riding, English or western. Shoulder-fore is a mild positioning of the shoulders just barely off the track, and it aligns the inside hind leg under the body. Ride it down every long side as a warm-up. Watch your horse get straighter over a month.

5. The stretchy circle

At the working jog or trot, on a 20-meter circle, gradually let the reins slide forward and ask your horse to stretch down into the bit. This is the gold standard for testing whether your horse is truly relaxed in the contact, or just compressed. If your horse won't stretch, you're holding him together with your hands, not your seat.

Trail-class exercises that fix bridle-pulling and braced jaws

The trail-class library is full of obstacles that solve specific western-horse problems. A few favorites:

  • Backing through an L — the horse who won't soften his jaw on a stop suddenly figures out softness when he physically can't back without it.
  • Side-passing along a pole — teaches the horse to move the body laterally with intent, not just drift.
  • Walking over a tarp — not just bombproofing; the horse who has to pay attention to footing stops rushing.

A 5-day western schedule with cross-training built in

You don't need to ride more — you need to ride differently 2 days a week. Sample schedule for a working-amateur western horse:

  • Monday: Discipline-specific (reining patterns, pleasure rail work, ranch-style maneuvers).
  • Tuesday: English flatwork day. Warm up, then 15 minutes of leg yield + shoulder-fore + stretchy circles. Cool down at the western jog.
  • Wednesday: Trail / obstacles. Two or three trail-class exercises.
  • Thursday: Off, or hand-walk.
  • Friday: Discipline-specific.
  • Saturday: English flatwork day. Transitions at the letter + 20-meter circles in both directions.
  • Sunday: Trail ride or lesson.

Two cross-training days per week is enough. The Tuesday and Saturday sessions fix the things your Monday/Friday discipline rides expose: drift, lack of bend, jaw bracing. Without cross-training, those problems compound. With cross-training, they get smaller every week.

What to ride in

For the flatwork days, ride in whatever feels right — a snaffle bit gives you the most direct feel for these exercises, but a well-fitted western curb works for most riders comfortable with it. The exercises are the point, not the tack. Don't let "I don't own a snaffle bridle" become an excuse to skip the cross-training.
