Building Confidence Over Fences: A 4-Week Progression for Horse and Rider

By Schneider Saddlery • May 27, 2026 • 9 min read

Confidence over fences is built on the flat. Not over fences. Read that twice.

The most common mistake riders make after a fall, a layoff, or moving up to a new height is to immediately try to jump their way back to confidence. That almost never works. What works is going back to the flatwork that controls the canter, the line, and the balance. Then introducing poles. Then introducing one small fence at a time. Then, eventually, a course.

This is the 4-week progression we recommend for any horse-and-rider pair that needs to rebuild confidence over fences. It works for the green horse seeing fences for the first time. It works for the seasoned horse coming back from a tendon rehab. It works for the rider who just took a fall and is white-knuckling every approach.

Before you start: the honest assessment

Three questions to answer before week one:

  1. Is your horse sound? If there's any doubt, vet first. A lameness that surfaces during work over fences is a much harder problem than a lameness caught on the flat.
  2. Is your canter rideable? Can you adjust the canter stride at will — lengthen, shorten, hold — on a 20-meter circle? If not, that's week one's real focus, not jumping.
  3. Are you riding alone or with eyes on the ground? The progression below is designed to be safe ridden alone, but if you can ride at least the first session of each week with a trainer watching, do it.

Week 1 — Flatwork only. No jumps.

This is the week where your jumping rebuild lives or dies. The goal: an adjustable canter, a horse who waits for your aids, and a rider whose body is in balance.

Ride three sessions this week. Each session focuses on:

  • Transitions within the canter — shortening and lengthening on a 20-meter circle. 6–8 transitions per side.
  • Canter to trot, trot to canter — at specific letters, not "somewhere on the long side."
  • Counter-bend exercises — these are the canter pirouette of straightness work. If your horse drifts on the approach to a fence, this fixes it.

  • Flatwork balance exercises
  • Flatwork straightness exercises

End week 1 with this test: ride a 20-meter circle at the canter, shorten the stride for half the circle, lengthen for the other half. Can you do it on both leads, smoothly, on the correct lead, without the horse breaking gait? If yes, proceed to week 2. If no, ride week 1 for another week.

Week 2 — Poles. Lots of poles.

This is the week your horse learns (or remembers) that ground poles are not scary. It's also the week your eye for distances starts to come back.

Three sessions this week. Each one builds on poles:

  • Trot poles — 4 poles, 4.5 feet apart, on a straight line. Ride through them in both directions. Then on a circle.
  • Canter poles — 3 poles, 9 feet apart for a quiet canter or 10 feet for a forward canter. Ride them in both directions.
  • Pole patterns — raised poles, a pole on a curve, a fan of poles. The goal is variety, not difficulty.

Week 2 exercise picks →

  • Beginner jumping exercises (lots of pole work)
  • Jumping exercises focused on rhythm

Watch yourself this week. Most riders rush their approach to poles even though they know poles are safe. If you find yourself doing it, that's information — that's the same tension you bring to a fence. Practice on poles is practice for fences.

Week 3 — One small fence at a time.

Now you start to jump. Note the singular: one small fence at a time. Not a course. Not even two jumps in a line. One fence, ridden many times.

Pick a fence at a height you and your horse are completely confident with — if you usually jump 2'6", set this at 2'0". If you usually jump 3', set this at 2'3". You want to be under-fenced, on purpose.

  • Session 1: One vertical at the easy height. Trot in, canter out. Ride it 6–8 times each direction. Focus on a quiet release and a balanced canter on the landing.
  • Session 2: Same vertical, canter in, canter out. Then introduce a second single fence elsewhere in the arena. Don't connect them yet.
  • Session 3: Two single fences, ridden as singles. Walk between. Focus on the approach line, not the jump.

  • Beginner confidence-building jumping exercises
  • Jumping exercises focused on approach

Week 4 — Gymnastics, then a small course.

By week 4 you and your horse should both feel that single fences are easy. Now you build the next layer: rhythm through a related distance, and the mental load of remembering a course.

  • Session 1: A simple gymnastic line — placement pole, two strides, small vertical, one stride, small vertical. Trot in, canter through. Ride it 5–6 times.
  • Session 2: A bounce or one-stride combination at a comfortable height. Same principle: rhythm matters more than height.
  • Session 3: A 6–8 fence course at the easy height. Walk it on foot first. Ride it once. Walk it again. Ride it a second time, focusing on the lines you couldn't quite hold the first time.

The mental side

Three tools that work for the rider's confidence, not the horse's:

  • Visualization. The night before a ride, visualize the exact sequence of exercises. See the line. See your horse's ears between yours. See the takeoff and landing. This is not a luxury; it's how your nervous system rehearses the ride.
  • Breath. Most riders hold their breath three strides out from a fence. Practice exhaling on the last stride. It changes how your body absorbs the takeoff.
  • Walk the course. Even if it's your own arena. Even if you set the jumps yourself. Walking the course locks the lines into your body before you ride them.

If something goes wrong mid-progression

Drop a level. If week 3 felt sketchy, repeat week 2. If week 2 felt sketchy, repeat week 1. The progression rewards patience; it punishes rushing. The riders who try to do this in two weeks instead of four are the ones who end up needing another rebuild three months later.
